# Forward Deployed Engineering, Medplum-Style

Forward Deployed Engineering is the hottest new role in tech. This post goes over the Forward Deployed Engineering role, and
the flavor of Forward Deployed Engineering we do at Medplum.

## What is a Forward Deployed Engineer?

Forward Deployed Engineers (FDEs) were first coined by Palantir, and initially, these engineers were literally deployed at
military bases to build and deploy software. Over time, other companies like Scale and C3 AI adopted the title for
engineers that worked directly work with customers, discovering that engineering talent that could communicate with and often work
directly alongside customers to implement on-the-ground solutions lead to better solutions for customers and better product feedback.

### Why has FDE become so popular?

In the last couple of years, AI has made building software easier and easier, shifting the bottleneck for growth from building
products themselves to being able to tightly deliver solutions customers will use. Players from Open AI and Anthropic to
Ramp and ElevenLabs have adopted FDE functions in order to speed up product discovery and deployment.

### What are we looking for in an FDE?

While enterprise buyers today may see the Forward Deployed model as a novel new implementation pattern, healthtech buyers have
long expected white-glove style deployments. Judy Faulkner, founder of Epic Systems and largest player in the electronic health
record (EHR) space, famously founded the company in 1979 by building one-off solutions for physician groups before launching
their flagship product, EpicCare, in 1992. To this day, Epic continues require developers to go on "immersion trips" to hospitals,
having them sit directly in patient visits and observe clinicians using their products.

We don't have any product managers at Medplum (as of this post!), and as such FDEs are responsible for bringing customer requests back and
helping set the tone of our product roadmap.

One concept the Medplum FDE team strives to inherit from our customers: bedside manner.

In a nutshell, bedside manner is a clinician’s approach towards a patient: jargon-less communication, respecting time and privacy, active listening, respectful and warm nonverbal cues. This allows for a better clinician-patient relationship, with clearer communication, more trust and honesty, and higher satisfaction with care.

While the stakes of good FDE bedside manner are not nearly as high, building trust with customers is an essential part of the FDE job. Let me be clear: this does not mean that Medplum FDE’s are not expected to have engineering fluency. Clinicians must be both knowledgeable and have good bedside manner to be effective. However, we explicitly train our FDE’s, many of whom have pure software engineering backgrounds, in these skills in order to build that extra level of trust with customers.

Forward Deployed Engineering was first coined by Palantir, and initally, these engineers were literally deployed at military
bases to build and deploy software. Over time, other companies like Scale and C3 AI adopted the title for
engineers deployed to work directly with customers, discovering that they needed talent who had solid
technical foundations, but more importantly excellent communication skills, to get products deployed
at large enterprises.

In the last couple years, AI has made building software easier and easier, and the bottleneck for growth has shifted from
products themselves to deploying software, especially in the enterprise market. Players from Open AI and Anthropic to Databricks and Ramp
and Decagon and ElevenLabs have implemented FDE functions in order to overcome this very hurdle.
[Leo at Ramp](https://ramp.com/fde) talks about this extensively in his role on the subject.

Healthcare is its own beast, and healthtech in particular has developed some unique patterns. While
the FDE model has only recently caught on in the tech market, the largest healthtech players like
Epic Systems were built with customer-facing software engineering at its core.

Judy Faulkner started the foundations for Epic Systems in 1979 by building a system to track patient data
over time for a group of physicians. For the first decade or so, Faulkner and her team continued to build one-off
solutions for physician groups, using this feedback to eventually launch
EpicCare, its first EMR, in 1992. The philosophy of designing software using time spent in the doctor's office
continues to this day.
To this day, Epic Systems continues to operate in a similar manner, famously
requiring that engineers embark on "immersion trips", spending time inside of hospitals, next to doctors
during patients visits, to learn how their software is being used.

As such, the FDE function is almost expected in healthcare: systems are complex and bureaucratic, and healthcare buyers see the
largest players in the game sending engineers to their offices. As such, Medplum must do the same.
